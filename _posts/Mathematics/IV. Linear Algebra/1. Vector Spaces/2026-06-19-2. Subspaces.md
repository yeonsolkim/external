**Definition 1.2.1.** A subset $\mathbb W$ of a vector space $\mathbb V$ over a field $\mathbb F$ is called a *subspace* of $\mathbb V$ if $\mathbb W$ is a vector space over $\mathbb F$ with the operations of addition and scalar multiplication defined on $\mathbb V.$<br>
<br>

**Remark 1.2.2.** Let $\mathbb V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb F,$ and $\mathbb W$ a subset of $V.$ Because (VS1), (VS2), (VS5), (VS6), (VS7), and (VS8) hold for all vectors in $\mathbb V,$ these automatically hold for the vectors in $\mathbb W.$ Thus $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V$ if and only if the following four properties are true in $\mathbb W.$ 
1. $\forall u,v\in \mathbb W \ (u+v\in \mathbb W).$
2. $\forall c \in \mathbb F\  \forall u\in \mathbb W \ (cu\in \mathbb W).$
3. $\exists u\in \mathbb W \ \forall v\in \mathbb W \ (v+u=v).$ 
4. $\forall u\in \mathbb W \ (-u\in \mathbb W).$

<br>

**Theorem 1.2.3.** Let $\mathbb V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb F,$ and $\mathbb W$ a subset of $V.$ Then $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V$ if and only if the following three properties are true in $\mathbb W.$
1. $\mathit 0\in \mathbb W.$
2. $\forall u,v\in \mathbb W \ (u+v\in \mathbb W).$
3. $\forall c \in \mathbb F\  \forall u\in \mathbb W \ (cu\in \mathbb W).$

*Proof*. Suppose that $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V.$ By Definition 1.2.1, (2) and (3) hold, and there exists $u\in \mathbb W$ such that $\forall v\in \mathbb W \ (v+u=v).$ But in $\mathbb V,$  we have $v+\mathit 0= v,$ and thus, by Theorem 1.1.9, $u=\mathit 0.$ Therefore (1) holds.<br>
&emsp; Conversely, suppose that (1), (2), and (3) hold. Then $(-1)u\in \mathbb W.$ We also have $0u=\mathit 0,$ because $u+0u=1u+0u=(1+0)u=1u=u.$ Thus $u+(-1)u=1u+(-1)u=(1+(-1))u=0u=\mathit 0.$ Therefore $-u=(-1)u\in \mathbb W.$ From Remark 1.2.2, we can see that $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V.$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Example 1.2.4.** The *transpose* $A^{{\!\top}}$ of an $m\times n$ matrix $A$ is the $n\times m$ matrix defined as $(A^{\!\top})_{ij}=A_{ji}.$ A *symmetric matrix* is a matrix $A$ such that $A^{\!\top}=A.$ It is clear that a symmetric matrix must be square, because when we suppose that $A$ is a $m\times n$ matrix where $m<n$ without loss of generality, there is no entries in $A^{\!\top}$ corresponding to the entries $A_{in}.$<br>
&emsp; The set $\mathbb W$ of all symmetric matrix in ${\mathbb F}^{n\times n}$ is a subspace of ${\mathbb F}^{n\times n}$ since Theorem 1.2.3 hold: 
1. Since $O^{\!\top}=O,$ $O\in \mathbb W.$
2. Let $A,B\in \mathbb W.$ We have $A_{ij}=A_{ji}$ and $B_{ij}=B_{ji},$ thus $(A+B)_{ij}=A_{ij}+B_{ij}=A_{ji}+B_{ji}=(A+B)_{ji}.$ Therefore $(A+B)\in \mathbb W.$
3. Let $A\in \mathbb W$ and $c\in \mathbb F.$ We have $A_{ij}=A_{ji},$ thus $(cA)_{ij}=cA_{ij}=cA_{ji}=(cA)_{ij}.$ Therefore $cA\in \mathbb W.$

<br>

**Example 1.2.5.** Let $n$ be a nonnegative integer, and let ${\mathbb F}_n[x]$ consist of all polynomials in ${\mathbb F}[x]$ having degree less than or equal to $n.$ 
1. Since the zero polynomial $\mathit 0\in {\mathbb F}[x]$ has degree $-\infty,$ it is in ${\mathbb F}_n[x].$
2. Let $P, Q \in {\mathbb F}[x].$ Since $\text{deg}(P+Q)\leq \max{\lbrace  \text{deg}(P),\text{deg}(Q)\rbrace }\leq n,\ (P+Q)\in {\mathbb F}_n[x].$ 
3. Let $P \in {\mathbb F}[x].$ Since $\text{deg}(cP)=\text{deg}(P)\leq n, \ cP\in {\mathbb F}_n[x].$

It therefore follows from Theorem 1.2.3 that ${\mathbb F}_n[x]$ is a subspace of ${\mathbb F}[x].$<br>
<br>

**Example 1.2.6.** Let $C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R)$ denote the set of all continuous real-valued functions defined on $\mathbb R.$ 
1. Since constant functions are continuous functions, $\mathit 0 \in C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R).$
2. Let $f,g\in C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R).$ Since $f+g$ is continuous function, $(f+g)\in C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R).$
3. Let $f\in C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R)$ and $c\in \mathbb R.$ Since $cf$ is a continuous function, $cf\in C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R).$

It therefore follows from Theorem 1.2.3 that $C(\mathbb R, \mathbb R)$ is a subspace of ${\mathbb F}[x].$<br>
<br>

**Example 1.2.7.** An $n\times n$ matrix $M$ is called *diagonal matrix* if $M_{ij}=0$ whenever $i\neq j,$ that is, if all its non-diagonal entries are zero. Let $\mathbb W$ denote the set of all $n\times n$ diagonal matrices.
1. Since $O_{ij}=0$ for all $i$ and $j,\ O\in \mathbb W.$
2. Let $A,B\in \mathbb W.$ If $i\neq j$ then $(A+B)_{ij}=A_{ij}+B_{ij}=0+0=0.$ Thus $(A+B)\in \mathbb W.$
3. Let $A\in \mathbb W$ and $c\in \mathbb F.$ If $i\neq j$ then $(cA)_{ij}=cA_{ij}=c0=0c=0.$ Thus $cA\in \mathbb W.$

It therefore follows from Theorem 1.2.3 that $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of ${\mathbb F}^{n\times n}.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 1.2.8.** Any intersection of subspaces of a vector space $\mathbb V$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V.$<br>
