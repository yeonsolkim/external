**Definition 1.1.1.** Let $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{field}}$ be the first-order language $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{field}} = \lbrace 0, 1, +, -, \cdot\rbrace,$ where $0$ and $1$ are constant symbols, $+$ and $\cdot$ are binary function symbols, and $-$ is a unary function symbol.<br>
<br>

**Definition 1.1.2.** Let $T_{\mathrm{field}}$ be the $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{field}}$-theory which consists of the following axioms:

1. $\forall x,y,z \ ((x + y) + z = x + (y + z)),$
2. $\forall x,y \ (x + y = y + x),$
3. $\forall x \ (x + 0 = x),$
4. $\forall x \ (x + (-x) = 0),$
5. $\forall x,y,z \ ((x \cdot y) \cdot z = x \cdot (y \cdot z)),$
6. $\forall x,y \ (x \cdot y = y \cdot x),$
7. $\forall x \ (x \cdot 1 = x),$
8. $\forall x,y,z \ (x \cdot (y + z) = (x \cdot y) + (x \cdot z)),$
9. $0 \neq 1,$
10. $\forall x \ (x \neq 0 \rightarrow \exists y \ (x \cdot y = 1)).$
{:marker-prefix="F" }

<br>

**Proposition 1.1.3.** For all $x,y,z\in \mathbb R,$ 
1. $x+y=x+z\Rightarrow y=z,$
2. $x+y=x\Rightarrow y=0,$ 
3. $x+y=0\Rightarrow y=-x,$
4. $-(-x)=x,$

*Proof.*<br>
(1) Since $x+y=x+z,$ $(x+y)+(-x) = (x+z) + (-x).$ Hence $y=z.$<br>
(2) Since $x+0=x,$ we have $x+y=x+0.$ Hence $y=0,$ by (1).<br>
(3) Since $x+(-x)=0,$ we have $x+y=x+(-x).$ Hence $y=-x,$ by (1).<br>
(4) Since $x+(-x)=0,$ $-(-x)=x,$ by (3).<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Proposition 1.1.4.** For all $x,y,z\in \mathbb R,$ 
1. $(x\ne 0 \land x\cdot y=x\cdot z)\Rightarrow y=z,$
2. $(x\ne 0 \land x\cdot y=x)\Rightarrow y=1,$
3. $x\cdot y=(-x)\cdot (-y),$
4. $(-1)\cdot x = -x.$

*Proof.*<br>
(1) Let $x\cdot w=1.$ Since $(x\cdot y)\cdot w = (x\cdot z)\cdot w,$ $(x\cdot w)\cdot y = (x\cdot w)\cdot z.$ Hence $y=z.$<br>
(2) Since $x\cdot 1 = x,$ $x\cdot y = x\cdot 1.$ Since $x\ne 0,$ by (1), $y=1.$<br>
(3) We have $x\cdot y+(-x)\cdot y=0.$ Then, $(-x)\cdot y+(-x)\cdot (-y)=0.$ Since the additive inverse of $(-x)\cdot y$ is unique, $x\cdot y=(-x)\cdot (-y).$<br>
(4) Since $x+((-1)\cdot x)=(1+(-1))\cdot x = 0,$ by (3) in Proposition 1.1.3, $(-1)\cdot x = -x.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Definition 1.1.5.** $0$ and $1$ are called the *additive identity* and *multiplicative identity*, respectively. $-x$ is called the *additive inverse* of $x$ or the *negative* of $x.$ <br>
&emsp; We write $y=1/x,$ if $x\ne 0$ and $x\cdot y = 1.$ Further, we often write 

$$ x-y,xy,\frac x y,2x,3x,x^2,x^3,\dots $$ 

in place of 

$$ x+(-y),x\cdot y,x\cdot \left(\frac 1 y \right),x+x,x+x+x,x\cdot x,x\cdot x\cdot x,\dots .$$

&emsp; An $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{field}}$-structure ${\mathcal F}=(\mathbb F, 0, 1, +, -, \cdot)$ is called a *field* if ${\mathcal F}\vDash T_{\mathrm{field}},$ and $x$ is called a *scalar* if $x\in {\mathbb F}.$<br>
<br>

**Definition 1.1.6.** Let $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{of}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{field}} \cup \{<\},$ where $<$ is a binary relation symbol, and let $T_{\mathrm{of}}$ be the $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{of}}$-theory that consists of the field axioms together with the following five axioms: 

1. $\forall x \ \neg(x < x),$
2. $\forall x,y,z\ ((x < y \land y < z) \rightarrow x < z),$
3. $\forall x,y\ (x\ne y \rightarrow (x < y \lor y < x)),$
4. $\forall x,y,z\ (x < y \rightarrow x + z < y + z),$
5. $\forall x,y,z\ ((x < y \land 0 < z) \rightarrow x\cdot z < y \cdot z).$
{:marker-prefix="OF" start="11"}

An $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{of}}$-structure $\mathcal{F} = (\mathbb{F}, 0, 1, +, -, \cdot, <)$ is called an *ordered field* if $\mathcal{F} \vDash T_{\mathrm{of}}.$<br>
<br>

**Proposition 1.1.7.** For all $w,x,y,z\in \mathbb R,$ 
1. $0<x \Rightarrow -x<0,$
2. $(x<0\land y<0) \Rightarrow 0<xy,$
3. $(x<y\land z<w) \Rightarrow x+z<y+w,$
4. $0\le x^2.$

*Proof.*<br>
(1) Since $x+(-x)=0,$ we have $x+(-x)<x.$ Then $(x+(-x))+(-x)<x+(-x)$ by (OF14) in Definition 1.1.6. Hence $-x<0.$<br>
(2) We have $0<x.$ Since $0<y,$ it follows that $0y<xy.$ But we also have the relation between $0$ and $0y,$ $0=0y.$ Therefore $0<xy.$<br>
(3) Since $x<y,$ it follows that $x+z<y+z.$ Similarly, since $z<w,$ we have  $z+y<w+y.$ Since the order relation is transitive, $x+z<w+y.$<br>
(4) Since the order relation is comparable if $0\ne x$ then $0<x$ or $x<0.$ If $0<x$ then $0x<xx,$ so that $0<x^2.$ If $x<0$ then $0<x^2.$ The only case left is that $x=0.$ If $x=0$ then $x^2=0x=0.$ Therefore $0\le x^2.$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Theorem 1.1.8.** The real number system $\mathcal R=(\mathbb R,0,1,+,-,\cdot,<)$ has the least-upper-bound property. Also, $\mathcal R$ is an ordered field. Equivalently,

$$
\mathcal R \vDash T_{\mathrm{of}}.
$$

&emsp; We henceforth identify $\mathbb N$, $\mathbb Z$, and $\mathbb Q$ with their respective images $\mathbb N^{\ast}$, $\mathbb Z^{\ast}$, and $\mathbb Q^{\ast}$ in $\mathbb R.$ Under this identification, we have

$$ \mathbb N \subseteq \mathbb Z \subseteq \mathbb Q \subseteq \mathbb R.$$

In particular, the rational field $\mathcal Q=(\mathbb Q,0,1,+,-,\cdot,<)$ is a subfield of $\mathcal R.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 1.1.9.** For all $x\in \mathbb R$ and all $y\in \mathbb R_{>0},$ there exists $n\in \mathbb N$ such that 

$$x<ny.$$

*Proof.* Let $x\in \mathbb R$ and $y\in\mathbb R_{>0}$ be given, and let $S$ be the set of all $ny,$ where $n$ runs through the positive integers. That is, 

$$ 
S = \lbrace ny : n\in \mathbb N\rbrace.
$$ 

We suppose, for contradiction, that $S$ is bounded above. Then $S$ has the supremum in $\mathbb R,$ by Theorem 1.1.8. Put $\alpha = \sup S.$ There is an element $my$ such that $\alpha - y < my.$ But then we have $\alpha < (m+1)y\in S,$ which contradicts that $\alpha$ is an upper bound for $S.$ Therefore it is concluded that the Theorem 1.1.9 is in fact true. <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Theorem 1.1.10.** For all $x,y\in \mathbb R,$ if $x<y$ then there exists $p\in \mathbb Q$ such that 

$$x<p<y.$$

*Proof.* Let $x$ and $y$ be reals such that $x<y.$ There exists $n\in \mathbb N$ such that $1<n(y-x),$ by Theorem 1.1.9. Hence we can choose such a number and denote it by $n.$ Then we consider the set

$$ S = \lbrace k \in \mathbb Z : nx < k\rbrace.$$

Theorem 1.1.9 implies that $S$ is nonempty. It is also bounded below, so $S$ has the least element; denote it by $m.$ Then we have 

$$ nx < m \le nx + 1<ny.$$ 

Since $0<n$ it follows that 

$$ x < \frac m n < y.$$ 

This proves Theorem 1.1.10. <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Theorem 1.1.11.** For all $y\in \mathbb R_{>0}$ and all $n\in \mathbb N,$ there exists the unique $x\in \mathbb R_{>0}$ such that 

$$ x^n = y.$$ 

*Proof*. It is clear that there is at most one such $x,$ since $0<x_1<x_2$ implies $x_1^n<x_2^n.$<br>
&emsp; We consider the set 

$$ S = \lbrace x\in \mathbb R_{>0}: x^n<y\rbrace.$$

If $x=y/(1+y)$ then $0<x<1.$ Since $x^n<x<y,$ $x\in S.$ Thus $S$ is nonempty. If $x>1+y$ then $y<x<x^n,$ so that $x\notin S.$ Thus $1+y$ is an upper bound for $S.$ Therefore there exists $\alpha$ such that $\alpha = \sup S.$<br>
&emsp; We suppose, for contradiction, that $\alpha^n<y$ or $y<\alpha^n.$<br>
&emsp; We first assume $\alpha^n<y.$ Choose $h$ so that 

$$ 0< h< \min\left\lbrace  1,\frac{y-\alpha^n}{n(\alpha+1)^{n-1}}\right\rbrace.$$ 

Then 

$$ (\alpha + h)^n - {\alpha}^n < hn(\alpha + h)^{n-1}<hn(\alpha + 1)^{n-1} < y-{\alpha}^n.$$

Thus $(\alpha + h)^n < y,$ so that $\alpha + h \in S.$ Since $\alpha<\alpha+h,$ this result contradicts the fact that $\alpha$ is an upper bound for $S.$<br>
&emsp; We now assume $y<\alpha^n.$ Choose 

$$ h = \frac{\alpha^n-y}{n\alpha^{n-1}}.$$

Then $0<h<\alpha.$ If $\alpha - h \le x,$ we conclude that 

$$ \alpha^n - x^n \le \alpha^n - (\alpha - h)^n<hn\alpha^{n-1} = \alpha^n - y.$$ 

Thus $y<x^n$ so that $x\notin S.$ Since $\alpha -h<\alpha,$ this results contradicts the fact that $\alpha$ is the supremum for $S.$<br>
&emsp; Therefore $\alpha^n=y,$ and this prove Theorem 1.1.11.<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Corollary 1.1.12.** For all $x,y\in \mathbb R_{>0}$ and $n\in \mathbb N,$ 

$$ (xy)^{1/n} = x^{1/n} y^{1/n}.$$ 

*Proof.* Let $x,y\in \mathbb R_{>0}$ be given. We let $a=(xy)^{1/n}, b=x^{1/n},$ and $c=y^{1/n}.$ Then we have $a^n=xy, b^n=x,$ and $c^n=y.$ Hence $a^n=b^nc^n=(bc)^n,$ so that 

$$\left(a^n\right)^{1/n} = \left((bc)^n\right)^{1/n}$$

by the uniqueness assertion of Theorem 1.1.11. Since  

$$ \left(a^n\right)^{1/n} = a\quad \text{and} \quad \left((bc)^n\right)^{1/n}=bc,$$ 

it follows that $a=bc.$ By definition of $a,b,$ and $c,$ therefore, 

$$ (xy)^{1/n} = x^{1/n} y^{1/n}.\tag*{\(\square\)}$$

## Exercises

**Exercise 1.** Prove that for all $x,y\in \mathbb R,$ 

1. $0<1,$
2. $x<y \Rightarrow -y<-x,$
3. $(0<x \land 0<xy) \Rightarrow 0<y,$
4. $0<x<y \Rightarrow 1/y<1/x,$
5. $x<y \Rightarrow x<(x+y)/2<y.$

*Proof.*<br>
(1) We have $0\le 1\cdot 1.$ Since $1\cdot 1 = 1$ and $0\ne 1,$ $0<1.$<br>
(2) Suppose, for contradiction, that $-x\le -y.$ Since $x<y,$ it follows that $x+(-x)<y+(-y),$ which contradicts $x+(-x)=y+(-y).$ Hence $-y<-x.$<br>
(3) Suppose, for contradiction, that $y\le 0.$ If $y=0,$ then $x\cdot y = 0,$ which contradicts $0<xy.$ If $y<0,$ then $0<-y,$ by (1). Thus $0<x(-y).$ It yields $0<-(xy),$ since $x(-y)= -(xy).$ Therefore $xy<0,$ which contradicts $0<xy.$ Consequently, it is concluded that $0<y.$<br>
(4) If $1/x\le 1/y$ then $x(1/x)<y(1/x)\le y(1/y).$ If follows that $x(1/x)<y(1/y),$ which contradicts $x(1/x)=1=y(1/y).$ Therefore it is concluded that $1/y< 1/x.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
