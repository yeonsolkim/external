**Definition 5.1.1.** We define an $n$-dimensional vector $\mathbf a$ to be an $n$-tuple. That is, 

$$ \mathbf a = (a_1,a_2,\dots, a_n),$$

where $a_i\in \mathbb R$ for all $i\in \lbrace 1,\dots,n\rbrace.$ In this context any real number is called a scalar.<br>
&emsp; Two $n$-dimensional vectors 

$$ \mathbf a = (a_1,\dots, a_n) \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf b = (b_1,\dots,b_n) $$

are equivalent if and if only $a_i=b_i$ for all $i\in \lbrace 1,\dots,n\rbrace.$ If $c\in \mathbb R,$ we define two operations of *vector addition* and *scalar multiplication* as follows: 

$$ \mathbf a + \mathbf b = (a_1+b_1,\dots,a_n+b_n) \quad \text{and} \quad c\mathbf a = (ca_1,\dots,ca_n).$$

&emsp; If $a_i=0$ for all $i\in \lbrace 1,\dots,n\rbrace,$ then the vector is called the *zero vector*, denoted by $\mathbf 0.$ We also define the *additive inverse* of a vector $\mathbf a$ to be the vector $-\mathbf a$ such that 
$$ -\mathbf a = (-a_1,-a_2,\dots, -a_n).$$
The set of all $n$-dimensional vectors is denoted by $\mathbb R^n.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 5.1.2.** If $\mathbf a, \mathbf b,$ and $\mathbf c$ are $n$-dimensional vectors, and $c$ and $d$ are scalars, then 

1. $\mathbf a+\mathbf b = \mathbf b + \mathbf a,$
2. $(\mathbf a + \mathbf b) + \mathbf c = \mathbf a + (\mathbf b + \mathbf c),$
3. $\mathbf a + \mathbf 0 = \mathbf a,$
4. $\mathbf a + (-\mathbf a) = \mathbf 0,$
5. $1\mathbf a = \mathbf a,$
6. $(cd)\mathbf a = c(d\mathbf a),$
7. $c(\mathbf a + \mathbf b) = c\mathbf a + c\mathbf b,$
8. $(c+d)\mathbf a = c\mathbf a + d\mathbf a.$

<br>

**Definition 5.1.3.** Suppose that

$$ \mathbf a = (a_1,\dots, a_n) \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf b = (b_1,\dots,b_n), $$

and $\mathbf a \neq 0$ and $\mathbf b \neq 0.$ The *dot product* of $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$ is the scalar $\mathbf a \cdot \mathbf b$ defined by 

$$ \mathbf a \cdot \mathbf b = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + \cdots + a_nb_n.$$

We define the *magnitude* of $\mathbf a$ to be the scalar $|\mathbf a|$ such that 

$$ \|\mathbf a\| = \sqrt{\mathbf a \cdot \mathbf a}.$$

The *unit vector* in the direction of $\mathbf a$ is the vector $\mathbf a$ multiplied by $1/|\mathbf a|.$ We also define the *angle* between $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$ to be the scalar $\theta\in [0,\pi]$ such that 

$$\theta = \arccos {\left(\frac{\mathbf a \cdot \mathbf b}{\|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf b\|}\right)}.$$

<br>

**Theorem 5.1.4.** Let $\mathbf a, \mathbf b,$ and $\mathbf c$ be nonzero vectors of $n$-dimension, and let $c\neq 0$ be a scalar. Then, 

1. $\mathbf a \cdot \mathbf b = \mathbf b \cdot \mathbf a,$
2. $\mathbf a \cdot (\mathbf b + \mathbf c) = \mathbf a \cdot \mathbf b + \mathbf a \cdot \mathbf c,$
3. $(c\mathbf a)\cdot \mathbf b = c(\mathbf a \cdot \mathbf b) = \mathbf a\cdot (c\mathbf b).$

<br>

**Definition 5.1.5.** We define the *standard bases* of $n$-dimension as follows: 

$$ 
\mathbf e_1=(1,0,\dots,0),\quad

\mathbf e_2=(0,1,\dots,0),\quad

\dots,\quad

\mathbf e_n=(0,0,\dots,1).

$$

For a nonzero vector $\mathbf a = (a_1,\dots,a_n)\in \mathbb R^n,$ we define the $i$*-th direction angle* of $\mathbf a$ as the dot product of $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf e_i.$ That is,

$$ \theta_i = \arccos {\left(\frac{\mathbf a \cdot \mathbf e_i}{\|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf e_i\|}\right)} = \arccos {\left(\frac{\mathbf a_i}{\|\mathbf a\|}\right)}.$$

<br>

**Definition 5.1.6.** If $\mathbf a = (a_1,a_2,a_3)\neq \mathbf 0,$ we denote the direction angles of $\mathbf a$ as $\alpha, \beta,$ and $\gamma.$ Then, $\cos {\alpha}, \cos {\beta},$ and $\cos{\gamma}$ are called the *direction cosines* of $\mathbf a.$ In this case, it follows that 

$$ 
\cos\alpha=\frac{a_1}{\|\mathbf a\|},\qquad

\cos\beta=\frac{a_2}{\|\mathbf a\|},\qquad

\cos\gamma=\frac{a_3}{\|\mathbf a\|}.$$ 

We also have 

$$ \cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1.$$

Since 

$$
\begin{align}
\mathbf a 
&= (a_1,a_2,a_3)\\
&= \big( \|\mathbf a\|\cos \alpha, \|\mathbf a\| \cos \beta, \|\mathbf a\|\cos \gamma\big)\\
&=\|\mathbf a\|(\cos \alpha, \cos \beta, \cos \gamma),
\end{align}
$$

it follows that 

$$ \frac {1}{\|\mathbf a\|}\mathbf a = (\cos \alpha, \cos \beta, \cos \gamma).$$

Therefore the direction cosines of $\mathbf a$ are the components of the unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf a.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 5.1.7.** Let $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$ be nonzero vectors of $n$-dimension. Then, 

$$ |\mathbf a\cdot \mathbf b| \leq \|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf b\|.$$

*Proof.* It is the same to prove that 

$$ (a_1b_1 + \cdots + a_nb_n)^2\le (a_1^2+\cdots + a_n^2)(b_1^2+\cdots + b_n^2).$$
