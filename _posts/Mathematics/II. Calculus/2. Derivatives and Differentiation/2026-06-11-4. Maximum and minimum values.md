**Definition 2.4.1.** Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function and let $c\in D.$ If 

$$\forall x\in D\  f(c)\ge f(x) $$ 

then $f(c)$ is called the *global maximum value* of $f.$ Or, if 

$$\forall x\in D\ f(c)\le f(x) $$ 

then $f(c)$ is called the *global minimum value* of $f.$ And the global maximum and minimum values of $f$ are called *extreme values* of $f.$<br>
<br>

**Definition 2.4.2.** Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function and let $c\in D.$ If there exists an open interval $I\subseteq D$ such that 

$$ c\in I \qquad \text{and} \qquad \forall x\in I\ f(c)\ge f(x) $$ 

then $f(c)$ is called a *local maximum value* of $f.$ Or, if there exists an open interval $I\subseteq D$ such that 

$$ c\in I \qquad \text{and} \qquad \forall x\in I\ f(c)\le f(x) $$ 

then $f(c)$ is called a *local minimum value* of $f.$ And local maximum and minimum values of $f$ are called *local extreme values* of $f.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 2.4.3** (*The Extreme Value Theorem*). Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function. If $f$ is continuous on a closed interval $I,$ then $f$ attains both a global maximum value and a global minimum value in $I.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 2.3.4** (*Fermat's Theorem*). Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function and let $c\in D.$ If $f$ has a local maximum or minimum at $c$ and $f$ is differentiable at $c,$ then 

$$f'(c)=0.$$
