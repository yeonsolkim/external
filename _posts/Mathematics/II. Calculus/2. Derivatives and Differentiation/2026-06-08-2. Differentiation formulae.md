**Definition 2.2.1.** Let $I$ be an open interval in $\mathbb R.$ The *differentiation operator* $D$ is the operator that sends each differentiable function $f : I \to \mathbb R$ to its derivative $f'.$ In other words, $Df$ is the function defined by

$$
(Df)(x) = f'(x)
$$

for every $x$ in $I.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 2.2.2.** Let be $\mathbf c$ a constant function and $\mathrm{id}$ the identity function. Then
1. $D\mathbf c=\mathbf 0$
2. $D(\mathrm{id})=\mathbf 1$

<br>

**Theorem 2.2.3.** If $f,g$ are differentiable function and $\mathbf c$ is a constant function, then  
1. $D(f+g)=Df+Dg$
2. $D(f-g)=Df-Dg$
3. $D(\mathbf c f)=\mathbf c (Df)$ 
4. $D(fg)=(Df)g+f(Dg)$

<br>

**Theorem 2.2.4.** If $f,g$ are differentiable function, and $g(x)\ne 0,$ then 

$$
D\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)=\frac{(Df)g-f(Dg)}{g^2}.
$$

<br>

**Theorem 2.2.5.** 
1. $D(\sin)=\cos$
2. $D(\cos)=-\sin$
3. $D(\tan)=\sec^2$

<br>

**Theorem 2.2.6 (The Chain Rule).** Let $g$ be differentiable at $x,$ and let $f$ be differentiable at $g(x).$ Then 

$$D(f\circ g)=[(Df)\circ g](Dg).$$

*Proof.* Let $k=f\circ g.$ Then we have $D(f\circ g)=Dk.$ Thus $(D(f\circ g))(x)=k'(x).$ Let $\Delta_h = g(x+h)-g(x)$ and let $\phi$ be the function such that

$$
\phi(t)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{f(g(x)+t)-f(g(x))}{t}, & t\ne 0,\\
f'(g(x)), & t=0.
\end{cases}
$$

Then we have 

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\frac{f(g(x+h))-f(g(x))}{h}\\
&=\phi(\Delta_h) \times \frac{\Delta_h}{h}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Let $\varepsilon>0$ be given. Since $\phi$ is continuous at $0,$ there exists $\eta>0$ such that $0<\vert \Delta_h \vert < \eta \Rightarrow \left\vert \phi(\Delta_h)-\phi(0)\right\vert<\varepsilon.$ Since $g$ is continuous at $x,$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that $0<\vert h \vert <\delta \Rightarrow \vert \Delta_h\vert < \eta.$ Thus $0<\vert h \vert <\delta \Rightarrow \left\vert \phi(\Delta_h)-\phi(0)\right\vert<\varepsilon.$ By Definition 1.3.2, $\lim_{h \to 0}{\phi(\Delta_h)}=\phi(0)=f'(g(x)).$ Thus we have 

$$
\begin{aligned}
k'(x)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\left[\phi(\Delta_h) \times \frac{\Delta_h}{h}\right]}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\phi(\Delta_h)} \times \lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}}\\
&=f'(g(x))\times g'(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Finally, $(D(f\circ g))(x)=f'(g(x))\times g'(x)=[(Df)\circ g](x)\times (Dg)(x).$ Therefore $D(f\circ g)=[(Df)\circ g](Dg).$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>

## Exercises
**Exercise 1.** Prove that $D(f+g)=Df+Dg.$<br><br>
*Proof.*<br>
Claim 1. $\text{LHS}=f'+g'.$<br>
*Subproof.* We have $(Df)(x)=f'(x) \Rightarrow (Df)(x)+(Dg)(x)=f'(x)+(Dg)(x).$ And, also $(Dg)(x)=g'(x) \Rightarrow (Dg)(x)+f'(x)=g'(x)+f'(x).$ Thus $(Df)(x)+(Dg)(x)=f'(x)+g'(x).$ Therefore $\text{LHS}=Df+Dg=f'+g'.$<span class="qed">$\Diamond$</span><br>
Claim 2. $\text{RHS}=f'+g'.$<br>
*Subproof.* Let $f+g=k.$ We have  $(D(f+g))(x)=k'(x)$ and, $k(x)=(f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x).$ Since

$$ \begin{aligned}
\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\frac{(f(x+h)+g(x+h))-(f(x)+g(x))}{h}\\
&=\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} + \frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h},
\end{aligned}
$$

if follows that 

$$ \begin{aligned}
\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\left[\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} + \frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}\right]}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}} + \lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}}\\
&=f'(x)+g'(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus $(D(f+g))(x)=f'(x)+g'(x).$ Therefore $\text{RHS}=D(f+g)=f'+g'.$<span class="qed">$\Diamond$</span><br>
By Claim 1 and Claim 2, $\text{LHS}=\text{RHS}.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Exercise 2.** Prove that $D(fg)=(Df)g+f(Dg).$<br>
*Proof.* Let $fg=k.$ Then we have $D(fg)=Dk=k',$ and $k(x)=f(x)g(x).$ It follows that

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&= \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h)-f(x)g(x)}{h}\\
&= \frac{(f(x+h)-f(x))g(x+h)+f(x)g(x+h)-f(x)g(x)}{h}\\
&= \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}g(x+h)+\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}f(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus 

$$ \begin{aligned}
\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\left[\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}g(x+h)+\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}f(x)\right]}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}g(x+h)}+\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}f(x)}\\
&=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $D(fg)(x)=k'(x)=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x),$ and $D(fg)=(Df)g+f(Dg).$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Exercise 3.** Prove that $D(\sin)=\cos.$<br><br>
*Proof.* Let $f=\sin.$ Then

$$ \frac{f(x+h)-f)x)}{h}=\frac{\sin (x+h)-\sin x}{h}. $$

We have $\sin (x+h)=\sin x \cos h + \cos x \sin h.$ Thus 

$$ 
\begin{aligned}
f'(x)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{\sin (x+h)-\sin x}{h}}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{\sin x (\cos h - 1)}{h}} + \lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{\cos x\sin h}{h}}\\
&=\sin x \lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{ \cos h - 1}{h}} + \cos x \lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{\sin h}{h}}\\
&=\cos x.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $f'=\cos,$ and $D(\sin)=\cos.$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
