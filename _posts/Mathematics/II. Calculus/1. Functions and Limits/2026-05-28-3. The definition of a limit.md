**Definition 1.3.1.** Let $f$ be a real-valued function. We define a limit as follows:

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L\Longleftrightarrow \forall \varepsilon >0\ \exists \delta >0\ \left( 0<\vert x-a\vert <\ \delta \Rightarrow \vert f(x)-L\vert <\varepsilon\right).$$

This definition is called epsilon-delta definition, which is the most proof-friendly definition of a limit. Note that the naive definition of a limit we would use before and the epsilon-delta definition is not equivalent. But there are a number of different definitions of a limit, which are semantically different but equivalent to the epsilon-delta definition. For example, we can also define a limit as follows: "Let $W$ be an arbitrary neighborhood of $L.$ Then there exists $U,$ a punctured neighborhood of $a,$ such that $f(U)\subseteq W.$"<br>
<br>

**Remark 1.3.2.**  Note that $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ is not a mathematical object itself until it is proved that $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L.$<br>
<br>

**Example 1.3.3.** Prove that $\lim_{x\to 3}(4x-5)=7.$<br><br>
*Proof.* Let $\varepsilon>0$ be given, and choose $\delta = {\varepsilon}/{4}.$ Suppose that $0 < \vert x - 3\vert  < \delta.$ Then we have:

$$\vert (4x - 5) - 7\vert = 4\vert x - 3\vert .$$

Since $\vert x - 3\vert < \delta,$ it follows that:

$$4\vert x - 3\vert  < 4\delta = \varepsilon.$$

Thus, whenever $0 < \vert x - 3\vert  < \delta,$ we have $\vert (4x - 5) - 7\vert  < \varepsilon.$ Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was chosen arbitrarily, the result holds for all $\varepsilon > 0,$ and, by Definition 1.3.1, $\lim_{x \to 3} (4x-5) = 7.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Definition 1.3.4.** We define a *left-hand limit* as follows:

$$\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)=L\Longleftrightarrow \forall \varepsilon >0\ \exists \delta >0\ \left( a-\delta < x < a \Rightarrow \vert f(x)-L\vert <\varepsilon\right).$$

And, we define a *right-hand limit* as follows:

$$\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=L\Longleftrightarrow \forall \varepsilon >0\ \exists \delta >0\ \left( a < x < a+\delta \Rightarrow \vert f(x)-L\vert <\varepsilon\right).$$

<br>

**Theorem 1.3.5** (Limit Laws). Let $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ and $\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M.$ And, let $c\in \mathbb R$ be a constant.
1. $\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)+g(x)]=L+M$
2. $\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)-g(x)]=L-M$
3. $\lim_{x\to a}[c\cdot f(x)]=c\cdot L$
4. $\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)\cdot g(x)]=L\cdot M$
5. If $M\neq 0,$ then $\lim_{x\to a}\left[f(x)/g(x)\right] = L/M$

<br>

**Theorem 1.3.6.** Let $c\in \mathbb R$ be a constant and $n\in \mathbb N_{\geq 2}.$
1. $\lim_{x\to a}c=c.$
2. $\lim_{x\to a}x=a.$
3. $\lim_{x\to a}x^n=a^n.$
4. $\lim_{x\to a}\sqrt [n]x=\sqrt [n]a.$

<br>

**Definition 1.3.7.**

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=\infty\Longleftrightarrow \forall M >0\ \exists \delta >0\ \left( 0<\vert x-a\vert <\ \delta \Rightarrow f(x) > M\right).$$

<br>

**Example 1.3.8.** Prove that $\lim_{x\to 0}1/x^2=\infty.$<br><br>
*Proof.* Let $M>0$ be given, and choose $\delta = \sqrt{1/M}.$ Suppose that $\vert x \vert < \delta.$ Then we have:

$$ x^2<{\delta}^2=\frac 1 M \Rightarrow \frac {1} {x^2} > M. $$

Thus whenever $\vert x \vert < \delta,$ we have ${1}/{x^2} > M.$ Since $M>0$ was chosen arbitrarily, the result holds for all $M>0,$ and, by Definition 1.3.7, $\lim_{x\to 0}1/x^2=\infty.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Definition 1.3.9.**

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=-\infty\Longleftrightarrow \forall N <0\ \exists \delta >0\ \left( 0<\vert x-a\vert <\ \delta \Rightarrow f(x) < N\right).$$

<br>

**Theorem 1.3.10.** Let $f$ and $g$ be real-valued functions defined on a domain $D\subseteq\mathbb{R},$ and let $a$ be an accumulation point of $D.$ Suppose that there exists a punctured neighborhood $U$ of $a$ such that $x\in U \Rightarrow f(x)=g(x).$ Then, for every $L\in\mathbb{R},$

$$  
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L \Longleftrightarrow  
\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=L.  
$$

<br>

## Exercises

**Exercise 1.** For given $\varepsilon > 0,$ find $\delta$ such that $0<\vert x-2\vert <\delta \Rightarrow \vert 1 / x - 1 / 2\vert  <\varepsilon.$<br><br>
*Solution.* 일단 출력 오차가 입력 오차로 완전히 표현되는지를 봐야할 것 같다: 

$$ \left\vert \frac 1 x -\frac 1 2\right\vert = \frac{\vert x-2\vert}{2\vert x\vert }.$$

$\delta$를 제한하게 되면 input error의 boundary가 제한되어 $1/2\vert x \vert$의 boundary에도 제한이 생기긴 할 것이다. $1/2\vert x \vert$의 boundary의 경계값 $C$가 존재한다면 $C\cdot \delta\leq \varepsilon,$ 즉 $\delta\leq \varepsilon / C$를 만족하기만 하면 될 것이다. $\delta < 2$로 제한하면 적어도 $C$가 존재하게 되고, $\delta \leq 1.9,$ 또는 $\delta \leq 1$로 더 제한하면 boundary-value가 constant로 생김을 알 수 있다. 계산 편의를 위해 $\delta \leq 1$로 한다면 $1/2$로 잡히게 된다. 그러면 $\delta \cdot 1/2 \leq \varepsilon,$ 즉, $\delta \leq 2\varepsilon$을 만족하면 되는 것이다. 따라서 boundary-value-inequality가 다음처럼 된다:

$$ (\delta \leq 1) \wedge (\delta \leq 2\varepsilon).$$ 

이로부터 $\delta \leq \min \lbrace 1, 2\varepsilon \rbrace$이면 됨을 알 수 있고, $\delta = \min \lbrace 1, 2\varepsilon \rbrace$로 하나로 정할 수도 있겠다. <br>
<br>

**Exercise 2.** For given $\varepsilon > 0,$ find $\delta$ such that $0<\vert x-a\vert <\delta \Rightarrow \vert \sqrt x - \sqrt a \vert  <\varepsilon.$<br><br>
*Solution.* $\delta$를 제한함에 따라 input error의 boundary가 제한되어 output error의 boundary도 줄긴 할 것이다. 이를 확인하기 위해 algebraic manipulation을 하면 

$$\vert \sqrt x - \sqrt a \vert = \vert x - a \vert \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt x + \sqrt a}$$

가 되겠다. $\vert x-a\vert$과 $1/(\sqrt x + \sqrt a)$는 당연히 $\delta$로 bound되어 있기는 하겠다. variable $\delta$를 제한하기 전에 그 boundarie들은 정해지지 않는다. 그런데 $1/(\sqrt x + \sqrt a)$는 이미 constant boundary가 있다: 

$$ 0< \frac{1}{\sqrt x + \sqrt a} \leq \frac {1}{\sqrt a}. $$

그러면 boundary-value-inequality가 다음처럼 된다: 

$$ \delta \cdot \frac {1}{\sqrt a}\leq \varepsilon.$$

따라서  $\delta \leq \sqrt a \varepsilon$이면 됨을 알 수 있고, $\delta = \sqrt a \varepsilon$으로 하나로 정할 수도 있겠다.<br>
<br>

**Exercise 3.** Find the value of 

$$ \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2-x-2}{x-2}. $$

*Solution.* 어떤 $x$에 대해 출력값은 ${x^2-x-2}$를 ${x-2}$로 나눈 값이다. $2$ 근방에서 각각을 보면 둘 다 $0$에 가깝다. 하지만 둘은 같은 수가 아니다. 이를 확인하기 위해 numerator를 denominator로 표현해봐야겠다: 

$$ x^2-x-2 = (x-2)^2 + 3(x-2)=(x-2)(x+1).$$

어떤 $x$에 대해, quotient가 $1$이 아니라 정확히 $x+1$임을 확인하였다. Theorem 1.3.10에 의하여

$$ \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2-x-2}{x-2}=\lim_{x\to 2}{(x+1)}=3.$$

<br>

**Exercise 4.** Prove that 

$$ \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2-x-2}{x-2}=3. $$

using epsilon-delta argument.<br><br>
*Proof.* Let $\varepsilon>0$ be given, and choose $\delta = \varepsilon.$ Suppose that $0<\vert x-2\vert <\delta.$ Then we have

$$\begin{aligned}
\vert f(x)-3\vert 
&= \left \vert \frac{x^2-x-2}{x-2}-3\right\vert  \\
&= \vert x-2 \vert \\
&< \delta\\
&=\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}$$

Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was chosen arbitrarily, the result holds for all $\varepsilon > 0.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span> <br>
<br>

**Exercise 5.** Let $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ and $\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M.$ Prove that $\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)+g(x)]=L+M.$<br><br>
*Proof.* Let $W$ be an arbitrary punctured neighborhood of $L+M.$ There exists $\varepsilon>0$ such that $(L+M-\varepsilon, L+M+\varepsilon)\subseteq W.$ Let $V=(L-\varepsilon /2, L+\varepsilon /2)$ and $(M-\varepsilon /2, M+\varepsilon /2).$ Since $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ and $\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M,$ there exists $U_f$ such that $f(U_f)\subseteq V,$ and there exists $U_g$ such that $f(U_g)\subseteq T.$ Let $U=U_f\cap U_g.$ Then

$$f(U)+g(U)\subseteq V+T\subseteq W.$$

Therefore, by the definition of limit, $\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)+g(x)]=L+M.$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Exercise 6.** Let $a, L\in \mathbb R$ be constants. Prove that 

$$  \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L \Longrightarrow \lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)= \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=L $$

*Proof.* Let $\varepsilon$ be given. There exists $\delta$ such that $0<\vert x-a\vert <\ \delta \Rightarrow \vert f(x)-L\vert <\varepsilon.$ We have $a-\delta < x < a \Rightarrow 0<\vert x-a\vert <\ \delta.$ Thus, $a-\delta < x < a \Rightarrow \vert f(x)-L\vert <\varepsilon.$ Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was chosen arbitrarily, the result holds for all $\varepsilon > 0.$ Therefore, by Definition 1.3.4, $\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)=L.$ In the same way, $\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=L.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span>
