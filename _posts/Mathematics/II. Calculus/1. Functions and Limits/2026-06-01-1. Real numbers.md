**Remark 1.1.1.** We assume that the real number system is already constructed.<br>
<br>

**Definition 1.1.2.** Let $x\in \mathbb R.$ *Zero* is the number $0$ such that 

$$ \forall x\ (x+0=x).$$

*One* is the nonzero number $1$ such that 

$$ \forall x\ (x\cdot 1 = x).$$

The *negative* of $x,$ denoted by $-x,$ is the number such that 

$$x+(-x)=0.$$

If $x\ne0,$ the *reciprocal* of $x,$ denoted by $1/x,$ is the number such that 

$$x\cdot \left(\frac 1 x\right)=1.$$

<br>

**Theorem 1.1.3.** Let $<$ denote the order relation on $\mathbb R,$ and let $x,y,z\in \mathbb R.$
1. If $x<y$ then $x+z<y+z.$
2. If $x<y$ and $0<z,$ then $x\cdot z< y\cdot z.$

<br>

**Definition 1.1.4.** Let $x,y\in \mathbb R.$ We define the *subtraction operation* by the formula 

$$ x-y = x + (-y).$$

If $y\neq 0,$ the *quotient* $x/y$ is the product of $x$ and the reciprocal of $y.$ That is, 

$$\frac x y = x\cdot \left(\frac 1 y\right).$$

We also define $x$ to be *positive* if $0<x,$ and to be *negative* if $x<0.$ When we write $x^2$ for $x\cdot x$ and let $0\le x,$ then the *square root* of $x$ is the number $\sqrt x$ such that 

$$ (\sqrt x)^2 = x.$$

<br>

**Definition 1.1.5.** Let $x\in \mathbb R.$ We define the *absolute value* of $x$ as follows:

$$
\vert x\vert  =
\begin{cases}
x, & \text{if } 0 \le x,\\
-x, & \text{if } x < 0.
\end{cases}
$$

<br> 

**Theorem 1.1.6.** Let $x,y\in \mathbb R.$ 

1. $0 \le \vert x\vert .$
2. $\vert x\cdot y\vert  = \vert x\vert \cdot \vert y\vert.$
3. $\vert x+y\vert  \le \vert x\vert +\vert y\vert.$
4. $\vert x\vert ^2 = x^2.$
5. $\vert x\vert  = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0.$

<br>

**Theorem 1.1.7.** Let $x\in \mathbb R.$ Then 

1. $\sqrt{x^2}=\vert x\vert,$
2. $(\sqrt{x})^2=x$ if $0\le x.$

<br>

**Definition 1.1.8.** Let $x_1,\dots,x_n$ be positive. The *harmonic mean* of $x_1,\dots,x_n$ is the number $\text{HM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ defined by  

$$
	\text{HM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)= \left(\frac 1 n {\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^{-1}}\right)^{\!\!-1}.
	$$
	
The *geometric mean* of $x_1,\dots,x_n$ is the number $\text{GM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ defined by

$$
	\text{GM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)={\left(\prod_{i=1}^n x_i\right)}^{\!\!\frac 1 n}.
	$$

The *arithmetic mean* of $x_1,\dots,x_n$ is the number $\text{AM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ defined by 

$$
	\text{AM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)=\frac 1 n {\sum_{i=1}^nx_i}.
	$$

The *quadratic mean* of $x_1,\dots,x_n$ is the number $\text{QM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ defined by 

$$
	\text{QM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)=\left( {\frac{1}{n} {\sum_{i=1}^n x^2_i}}\right)^{\!\!\frac 1 2}.
	$$

An *weighted arithmetic mean* of $x_1,\dots,x_n$ is the number $\text{WAM}(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ defined by  

$$
	\text{WAM}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i x_i
	$$

where $0\le w_i$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i=1.$<br>
<br>

**Theorem 1.1.9.** Let $x_i\in \mathbb R$ be positive for all $i\in \lbrace 1,2,\dots,n\rbrace.$ Then

$$
\min(x_1,\ldots,x_n) \le \operatorname{mean}(x_1,\ldots,x_n) \le \max(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
$$

<br>

**Theorem 1.1.10.** Let $x_i\in \mathbb R$ be positive for all $i\in \lbrace 1,2,\dots,n\rbrace.$ Then 

$$
\text{HM}(x_1,\dots,x_n) \le \text{GM}(x_1,\dots,x_n) \le \text{AM}(x_1,\dots,x_n) \le \text{QM}(x_1,\dots,x_n).
$$

The equality holds if and only if $x_1 = \dots = x_n.$<br>
<br>

## Exercises
**Exercise 1.** Prove that if $x$ is positive then the negative of $x$ is negative.<br><br>
*Proof.* We recall Theorem 1.1.3: If there is a relation between $x$ and $y,$ then for any number $z,$ there is a relation between the sum of $x$ and $z,$ and the sum of $y$ and $z.$ Returning to the problem, we have the relation between the $0$ and $x,$ $0<x.$ We also have another binary relation such that $x+(-x)=0.$ Then we can compare $x+(-x)$ and $x,$ that is, $x+(-x)<x.$ Thus $(-x) + (x+(-x)) < x+(-x),$ so that $-x<0.$ Therefore the negative of $x$ is negative. <span class="qed">$\square$</span> <br>
<br>

**Exercise 2.** Prove that the product of two negative numbers $x$ and $y,$ namely $xy$ is positive.<br><br>
*Proof.* We have $0<x.$ Since $0<y,$ it follows that $0y<xy.$ But we also have the relation between $0$ and $0y,$ $0=0y.$ Therefore $0<xy,$ and $xy$ is positive.<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Exercise 3.** Prove that if $x<y$ and $z<w$ then $x+z<y+w.$<br><br>
*Proof.* Since $x<y,$ it follows that $x+z<y+z$ by Theorem 1.1.3. Similarly, since $z<w,$ we have  $z+y<w+y.$ Since the order relation is transitive, $x+z<w+y.$<span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Exercise 4.** Let $x\in \mathbb R.$ Prove that $0\le x^2.$<br><br>
*Proof.* Since the order relation is comparable if $0\ne x$ then $0<x$ or $x<0.$ If $0<x$ then $0x<xx,$ so that $0<x^2.$ If $x<0$ then $0<x^2$ holds from the consequence of Exercise 2. The only case left is that $x=0.$ If $x=0$ then $x^2=xx=0.$ Therefore $0\le x^2.$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span><br>
<br>

**Exercise 5.** Let $x\in \mathbb R.$ Prove that 

$$ 2\le\frac{x^2+2}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}.$$

*Proof.* We have

$$ \sqrt{(x^2+1)\cdot 1}\le \frac{(x^2+1)+1}{2}, $$  

by GM-AM inequality. It follows that 

$$ \sqrt{x^2+1}\le \frac{x^2+2}{2}\iff 2\le \frac{x^2+2}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}.\tag*{\(\square\)}$$

<br>

**Exercise 6** (Nesbitt's inequality). Let $a,b,$ and $c$ be positive numbers. Prove that 

$$ \frac 3 2 \le \frac{a}{b+c} + \frac{b}{a+c} + \frac{c}{a+b}. $$ 

*Proof.* We have 

$$ \left(\frac{(a+b)^{-1}+(b+c)^{-1}+(c+a)^{-1}}{3}\right)^{\!\!-1} \le \frac{(a+b)+(b+c)+(c+a)}{3},$$

by HM-AM inequality. Thus

$$ \frac 1 2 \le \left(\frac{a+b+c}{3}\right)\left(\frac{(a+b)^{-1}+(b+c)^{-1}+(c+a)^{-1}}{3}\right).$$

If follows that 

$$ \frac{3}{2} \le (a+b+c)\left(\frac{1}{a+b}+\frac{1}{b+c}+\frac{1}{c+a}\right) -3.$$

Therefore, 

$$ \frac 3 2 \le \frac{a}{b+c} + \frac{b}{a+c} + \frac{c}{a+b}.\tag*{\(\square\)}$$

<br>

**Exercise 7.** Let $a,b,$ and $c$ be real numbers. Prove that 

$$ a^2bc + b^2ca + c^2ab \le a^4+ b^4 + c^4.$$

*Proof.* We have 

$$ \frac{a+b+c}{3}\le \sqrt{\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2}{3}}, $$ 

by AM-QM inequality.  
Similarly, by AM-QM inequality, we also have 

$$ {\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2}{3}}\le \sqrt{\frac{a^4+b^4+c^4}{3}}.$$

Thus 

$$\left(\frac{a+b+c}{3}\right)^{\!\!4}\le \left( {\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2}{3}} \right)^{\!\!2} \le {\frac{a^4+b^4+c^4}{3}}.$$

Since 

$$ abc \le \left(\frac{a+b+c}{3}\right)^{\!\!3} $$

by GM-AM inequality, it follows that 

$$ abc\left(\frac{a+b+c}{3}\right) \le \left(\frac{a+b+c}{3}\right)^{\!\!4}\le {\frac{a^4+b^4+c^4}{3}}.$$

Therefore 

$$ a^2bc + b^2ca + c^2ab \le a^4+ b^4 + c^4. \tag*{\(\square\)} $$
