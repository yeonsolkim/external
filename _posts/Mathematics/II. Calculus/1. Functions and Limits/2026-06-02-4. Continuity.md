**Definition 4.1.** A function $f$ is *continuous* at $x=a$ if 

$$ \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a).$$

We say $f$ is *discontinuous* at $x=a$ if $f$ is not continuous at $x=a$.<br>
<br>

**Example 4.1.** Where is the following function discontinuous?

$$ f(x) = \left \lfloor x \right \rfloor. $$

**Solution.** floor function은 $x$보다 크지 않은 최대의 integer를 출력한다. 경향을 보기 위해 function plot을 그려보자. (See Figure 4.1.) discontinuous가 의심되는 지점은 어떤 $n\in \mathbb Z$에 대해 $x=n$인데, 여기서는  $\lim_{x\to n^-}f(x)+1=\lim_{x\to n^+}f(x)$가 되어 limit이 존재하지 않음을 알 수 있다.

**Definition 4.2.** A function $f$ is *continuous from the right* at $x=a$ if 

$$ \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=f(a),$$

and $f$ is *continuous from the left* at $x=a$ if 

$$ \lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)=f(a).$$

<br>

**Theorem 4.1.** If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $x=a$ and $c$ is a constant, then the following functions are also continuous at $x=a$.
1. $f+g$
2. $f-g$
3. $c\cdot f$
4. $f \cdot g$
5. $f/g$ if $g(a)\neq 0$.

<br>

**Definition 4.3.** A function $f$ is *continuous on an interval* if it is continuous at every $x$ in the interval. (If $f$ is defined only on one side of an endpoint of the interval, we understand continuous at the endpoint to mean continuous from the right or continuous from the left.)<br>
&emsp; It follows from this definition and Theorem 4.1 that if $f$ and $g$ are continuous on an interval, then so are the functions $f+g, f-g, c\cdot f, f\cdot g,$ and (if $g$ is never $0$) $f/g$.<br>
<br>

**Theorem 4.2.** The following types of functions are continuous at every number in their domains:
1. polynomial functions
2. rational functions
3. root functions
4. trigonometric functions

<br>

**Theorem 4.3.** If $g$ is continuous at $x=a$ and $f$ is continuous at $x=g(a)$, then $f\circ g$ is continuous at $x=a$.<br>
<br>

**Example 4.2.** Let $F : D \to \mathbb R$ be the function such that 

$$ F(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+7}-4} $$

for every input $x\in D$, where $D=\lbrace x\in \mathbb R : x\neq \pm 3\rbrace$. Where is $F$ continuous?
**Solution.** For every $x\in D$

$$F(x)=f(g(h(k(x)))=(f\circ g\circ h\circ k)(x)$$ 

where $f(x)=1/x, g(x)=x-4, h(x)=\sqrt x,$ and $k(x)=x^2+7$. That is, $F=f\circ g\circ h\circ k$. Because $f, g, h, k$ are continuous on $D$, $f\circ g\circ h\circ k$ is also continuous on $D$ by Theorem 4.3. Thus $F$ is continuous on $D$.<br>
<br>

**Theorem 4.4 (*the intermediate value theorem*).** Suppose that $f$ is continuous on the closed interval $[a,b]$ and let $k$ be any number between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, where $f(a)\neq f(b)$. Then there exists a number $c\in (a,b)$ such that $f(c)=k$.<br>

## Exercises
**Exercise 4.1.** Prove that the function $f(x)=1-\sqrt{1-x^2}$ is continuous on the interval $[-1,1]$.<br>
**Proof.** We first prove two elementary estimates.
> **Lemma 1.** Let $x\in[-1,1]$ and $a\in[0,1)$. Then
> 
> $$
> \frac{|x+a|}{\sqrt{1-x^2}+\sqrt{1-a^2}}
> \leq
> \sqrt{\frac{1+a}{1-a}}.
> $$
> 
> Moreover, equality holds if and only if $x=1$.<br>
> **Proof.** Since $a\in[0,1)$, we have $\sqrt{1-a^2}>0$, so the denominator is positive. Also, since $x\in[-1,1]$ and $a\geq 0$, we have $\vert x+a\vert \leq 1+a$. Therefore,
> 
> $$
> \frac{|x+a|}{\sqrt{1-x^2}+\sqrt{1-a^2}}
> \leq
> \frac{1+a}{\sqrt{1-a^2}}=\sqrt{\frac{1+a}{1-a}}.
> $$
> 
> Equality holds precisely when both inequalities used above are equalities. This requires $\vert x+a\vert =1+a$ and $\sqrt{1-x^2}=0$. The second condition gives $x=\pm 1$. If $x=-1$, then
> 
> $$
> |x+a|=|-1+a|=1-a<1+a,
> $$
> 
> because $a\in[0,1)$. Hence equality cannot occur at $x=-1$. Therefore equality holds if and only if $x=1$.<br>

> **Lemma 2.** Let $x\in[-1,1]$ and $a\in(-1,0)$. Then
> 
> $$
> \frac{|x+a|}{\sqrt{1-x^2}+\sqrt{1-a^2}}
> \leq
> \sqrt{\frac{1-a}{1+a}}.
> $$
> 
> Moreover, equality holds if and only if $x=-1$.<br>
> **Proof.** Since $a\in(-1,0)$, we have $\sqrt{1-a^2}>0$, so the denominator is positive. Also, since $x\in[-1,1]$ and $a<0$, we have $\vert x+a\vert \leq 1-a$. Therefore,
> 
> $$
> \frac{|x+a|}{\sqrt{1-x^2}+\sqrt{1-a^2}}
> \leq
> \frac{1-a}{\sqrt{1-a^2}}=\sqrt{\frac{1-a}{1+a}}.
> $$
> 
> Equality holds precisely when both inequalities used above are equalities. This requires $\vert x+a\vert =1-a$ and $\sqrt{1-x^2}=0$. The second condition gives $x=\pm 1$. If $x=1$, then
> 
> $$
> |x+a|=|1+a|=1+a<1-a,
> $$
> 
> because $a\in(-1,0)$. Hence equality cannot occur at $x=1$. Therefore equality holds if and only if $x=-1$.<br>

We now prove that $f$ is continuous on $[-1,1]$. We prove continuity relative to the domain $[-1,1]$. Let $a\in[-1,1]$ and let $\varepsilon>0$ be given. First suppose that $a\in(-1,1)$. If $a\in[0,1)$, choose

$$
\delta=\varepsilon\sqrt{\frac{1-a}{1+a}}.
$$

Suppose that $x\in[-1,1]$ and $\vert x-a\vert <\delta$. Then, by Lemma 1, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
|f(x)-f(a)|
&=
\left|
\left(1-\sqrt{1-x^2}\right)
-
\left(1-\sqrt{1-a^2}\right)
\right| \\
&=
\frac{|a^2-x^2|}
{\sqrt{1-x^2}+\sqrt{1-a^2}} \\
&<
\delta\sqrt{\frac{1+a}{1-a}} \\
&=
\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $f$ is continuous at $a$. If $a\in(-1,0)$, choose

$$
\delta=\varepsilon\sqrt{\frac{1+a}{1-a}}.
$$

Suppose that $x\in[-1,1]$ and $\vert x-a\vert <\delta$. Then, by Lemma 2, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
|f(x)-f(a)|
&=
\left|
\left(1-\sqrt{1-x^2}\right)
-
\left(1-\sqrt{1-a^2}\right)
\right| \\
&=
\frac{|a^2-x^2|}
{\sqrt{1-x^2}+\sqrt{1-a^2}} \\
&<
\delta\sqrt{\frac{1-a}{1+a}} \\
&=
\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $f$ is continuous at $a$. Thus $f$ is continuous at every point $a\in(-1,1)$.<br>
&emsp; It remains to check the endpoints. At $a=-1$, we have $f(-1)=1$. Let $\varepsilon>0$ be given. Choose

$$
\delta=\frac{\varepsilon^2}{2}.
$$

Suppose that $x\in[-1,1]$ and $\vert x-(-1)\vert =\vert x+1\vert <\delta.$ Since $x\in[-1,1]$, we have $x+1\geq 0$ and $1-x\leq 2$. Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
|f(x)-f(-1)|
&=
\left|1-\sqrt{1-x^2}-1\right| \\
&=
\sqrt{(1-x)(1+x)} \\
&\leq
\sqrt{2(1+x)} \\
&=
\sqrt{2|x+1|} \\
&<
\sqrt{2\delta} \\
&=
\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $f$ is continuous from the right at $-1$. At $a=1$, we have $f(1)=1$. Let $\varepsilon>0$ be given. Choose

$$
\delta=\frac{\varepsilon^2}{2}.
$$

Suppose that $x\in[-1,1]$ and $\vert x-1\vert <\delta$. Since $x\in[-1,1]$, we have $1-x\geq 0$ and $1+x\leq 2$. Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
|f(x)-f(1)|
&=
\left|1-\sqrt{1-x^2}-1\right| \\
&=
\sqrt{(1-x)(1+x)} \\
&\leq
\sqrt{2(1-x)} \\
&=
\sqrt{2|x-1|} \\
&<
\sqrt{2\delta} \\
&=
\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $f$ is continuous from the left at $1$. Hence, $f$ is continuous on $[-1,1]$. Q.E.D.<br>

**Exercises 4.2.** Prove that if functions $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $x=a$, then $f+g$ is also continuous at $x=a$.<br>
**Solution.** 먼저 continuous는 'when $x$ approaches to $a$, $f(x)$ approaches to $f(a)$'를 의미한다. 그리고 $f+g$는 input $x$에 대하여 $f(x)+g(x)$를 출력하는 function이다. 그렇다면 'when $x$ approaches to $a$, $f(x)+g(x)$ approaches to $f(a)+g(a)$'를 보이면 되는 것이다. 그런데 이는 자명하다: Because $f(x)$ approaches to $f(a)$ and $g(x)$ approaches to $g(a)$, $f(x)+g(x)$ approaches to $f(a)+g(a)$.<br>
<br>

**Exercise 4.3.** Prove that any polynomial is continuous everywhere.<br>
**Solution.** $f$가 polynomial function이라는 것의 의미는 'there exist numbers $n\in \mathbb N$ and $c_n,\dots, c_0\in \mathbb R$ such that for all input $x$, $f(x)=c_nx^n+c_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+c_1x+c_0$'이다. 그렇다면, $f$가 polynomial function이라면, 모든 $a\in \mathbb R$에 대하여 '$f$ is continuous at $x=a$'가 성립할까? 성립하며 이유는 다음과 같다: Theorem 3.1과 Theorem 3.2에 의하여

$$ \begin{aligned} \lim_{x\to a}f(x) 
&= \lim_{x\to a}(c_nx^n+c_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+c_1x+c_0) \\
&= \lim_{x\to a}c_n x^n + \lim_{x\to a}c_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + \lim_{x\to a}c_1x + \lim_{x\to a}c_0 \\
&= c_na^n + c_{n-1}a^{n-1} + \cdots + c_1 a + c_0 \\ 
&= f(a).
\end{aligned}$$
