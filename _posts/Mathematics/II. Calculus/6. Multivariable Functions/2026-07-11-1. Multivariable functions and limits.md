**Definition 6.1.1.** If $X_1,\ldots,X_n\subseteq \mathbb R$ and $D=X_1\times\cdots\times X_n,$ a function

$$f:D\longrightarrow \mathbb R.$$

is a *function of* $n$ *variables.*

<br>

**Definition 6.1.2.** Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function of two variables and let $k\in f(D).$ A *level curve* of $f$ is the curve with the equation $f(\mathbf x) = k.$ A collection of level curves is called a *contour map.*<br>
<br>

**Definition 6.1.3.** Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function of three variables and let $k\in f(D).$ A *level surface* of $f$ is the surface with the equation $f(\mathbf x) = k.$<br>
<br>

**Definition 6.1.4.** Let $f:D\to \mathbb R$ be a function of $n$ variables. We define a limit of $f$ as follows: 

$$\lim_{\mathbf  x\to\mathbf  a}f(\mathbf  x)=L\Longleftrightarrow \forall \varepsilon >0\ \exists \delta >0\ \forall \mathbf x\in D\ \left( 0<\|\mathbf  x-\mathbf a\| <\ \delta \Rightarrow | f(\mathbf x)-L| <\varepsilon\right).$$

The negation can be written as follows: 

$$
\forall L\in \mathbb R\ \exists \varepsilon >0\ \forall \delta >0\ \exists\mathbf  x\in D\ \left( 0<\|\mathbf  x-\mathbf a\| <\ \delta \land \varepsilon \le | f(\mathbf x)-L| \right).
$$

<br>

## Exercises

**Exercise 1.** Show that there is no $L\in \mathbb R$ such that 

$$ 
\lim_{(x,y)\to (0,0)} \frac{x^2-y^2}{x^2+y^2} = L.
$$

*Proof.* Let $L\in\mathbb R$ be arbitrary. We show that there exists $\varepsilon>0$ such that 

$$ 
\forall \delta>0\ \exists \mathbf x\in D \ \left( 0<\|\mathbf  x-\mathbf 0\| <\ \delta \land \varepsilon \le | f(\mathbf x)-L| \right).
$$

By the triangle inequality,

$$  
2=|1-(-1)|  
\le |1-L|+|-1-L|.  
$$

Hence

$$  
1\le|1-L|  
\qquad\text{or}\qquad  
1\le|-1-L|.
$$

Set $\varepsilon=1$, and let $\delta>0$ be arbitrary. If $1\le|1-L|,$ choose $\mathbf x =\left({\delta}/{2},0\right).$ Then

$$  
0<\|\mathbf  x-\mathbf 0\|=\frac{\delta}{2}<\delta  
\qquad\text{and}\qquad   
\varepsilon = 1\le|1-L|  .
$$

If instead $1\le|-1-L| ,$ choose $\mathbf x =\left(0,{\delta}/{2}\right).$ Then

$$  
0<\|\mathbf  x-\mathbf 0\|=\frac{\delta}{2}<\delta  
\qquad\text{and}\qquad  
\varepsilon = 1\le|-1-L|  .
$$

Thus, for every $L\in\mathbb R$, there exists $\varepsilon>0$ such that for every $\delta>0$, there exists $\mathbf x$ satisfying

$$  
0<\|\mathbf  x-\mathbf 0\|<\delta  
\qquad\text{and}\qquad 
\varepsilon\le
\left|  
f(\mathbf x)-L  
\right|  
$$

Therefore, there is no $L\in\mathbb R$ such that

$$  
\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}  
\frac{x^2-y^2}{x^2+y^2}=L.  \tag*{\(\square\)} 
$$
