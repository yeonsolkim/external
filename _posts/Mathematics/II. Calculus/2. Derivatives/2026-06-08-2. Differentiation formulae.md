**Definition 2.2.1.** Let $I$ be an open interval in $\mathbb R$. The *differentiation operator* $D$ is the operator that sends each differentiable function $f : I \to \mathbb R$ to its derivative $f'$. In other words, $Df$ is the function defined by

$$
(Df)(x) = f'(x)
$$

for every $x$ in $I$.<br>
<br>

**Theorem 2.2.2.** Let be $\mathbf c$ a constant function and $\mathrm{id}$ the identity function. Then
1. $D\mathbf c=\mathbf 0$
2. $D(\mathrm{id})=\mathbf 1$

<br>

**Theorem 2.2.3.** If $f,g$ are differentiable function and $\mathbf c$ is a constant function, then  
1. $D(f+g)=Df+Dg$
2. $D(f-g)=Df-Dg$
3. $D(\mathbf c f)=\mathbf c Df$ 
4. $D(fg)=(Df)g+f(Dg)$

<br>

**Theorem 2.2.4.** If $f,g$ are differentiable function, and $g(x)\ne 0$, then 

$$
D\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)=\frac{(Df)g-f(Dg)}{g^2}.
$$

<br>

## Exercises
**Exercise 2.2.1.** Prove that $D(f+g)=Df+Dg$.<br>
**Proof.**<br>
Claim 1. $\text{LHS}=f'+g'$.<br>
Proof of Claim 1. We have $(Df)(x)=f'(x) \Rightarrow (Df)(x)+(Dg)(x)=f'(x)+(Dg)(x)$. And, also $(Dg)(x)=g'(x) \Rightarrow (Dg)(x)+f'(x)=g'(x)+f'(x)$. Thus $(Df)(x)+(Dg)(x)=f'(x)+g'(x)$. Therefore $\text{LHS}=Df+Dg=f'+g'$.<br>
<br>

Claim 2. $\text{RHS}=f'+g'$.<br>
Proof of Claim 2. Let $f+g=k$. We have  $(D(f+g))(x)=k'(x)$ and, $k(x)=(f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)$. Since

$$ \begin{aligned}
\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\frac{(f(x+h)+g(x+h))-(f(x)+g(x))}{h}\\
&=\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} + \frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h},
\end{aligned}
$$

if follows that 

$$ \begin{aligned}
\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\left[\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} + \frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}\right]}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}} + \lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}}\\
&=f'(x)+g'(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus $(D(f+g))(x)=f'(x)+g'(x)$. Therefore $\text{RHS}=D(f+g)=f'+g'$.<br>
<br>

By Claim 1 and Claim 2, $\text{LHS}=\text{RHS}$. Q.E.D.<br>

**Exercise 2.2.2.** Prove that $D(fg)=(Df)g+f(Dg)$.<br>
**Proof.** Let $fg=k$. Then we have $D(fg)=Dk=k'$, and $k(x)=f(x)g(x)$. It follows that 

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&= \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h)-f(x)g(x)}{h}\\
&= \frac{(f(x+h)-f(x))g(x+h)+f(x)g(x+h)-f(x)g(x)}{h}\\
&= \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}g(x+h)+\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}f(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus 

$$ \begin{aligned}
\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{k(x+h)-k(x)}{h}
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\left[\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}g(x+h)+\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}f(x)\right]}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}g(x+h)}+\lim_{h\to 0}{\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}f(x)}\\
&=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $D(fg)(x)=k'(x)=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x)$, and $D(fg)=(Df)g+f(Dg)$. Q.E.D.
