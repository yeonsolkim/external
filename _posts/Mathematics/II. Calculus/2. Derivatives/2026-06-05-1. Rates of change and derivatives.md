**Definition 1.1** (*Rates of Change*). Suppose $y$ is a quantity that depends on another quantity $x$. If $x$ changes from $x_1$ to $x_2$, then the change is $\Delta x = x_2-x_1$ and the corresponding change in $y$ is $\Delta y=f(x_2)-f(x_1)$. The difference quotient 

$$ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}=\frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1} $$

is called the *average rate of change* of $y$ with respect to $x$ over the interval $[x_1,x_2]$.<br>
&emsp; Similarly, we define the *instantaneous rate of change* of $y$ with respect to $x$ at $x=a$ as follows: 

$$ \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right\vert_a=\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}.$$ 

This concept also can be seen with respect to functions. When we introduce a real-valued function $f$, it is called the *derivative* of $f$ at $x=a$ and denoted by $f'(a)$.
<br>

**Definition 1.2** (*Derivatives*). A function $f':D\to \mathbb R$ is the *derivative* of $f$ if there exists a function $f$ such that 

$$ f'(x)=\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$ 

for every input $x\in D$ where $D=\lbrace x\in \mathbb R : f'(x) \text{ exists}\rbrace$.
