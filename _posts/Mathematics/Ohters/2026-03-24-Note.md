**Injective.** $f(a_1)=f(a_2)\rightarrow a_1=a_2.$ This kind of functions are called an *injection* or *one-to-one function*.<br>
**Surjective.** Every element in the codomain has at least one element in the domain mapping to it; the range equals the codomain. This kind of functions are called a *surjection* or *onto function*.<br>
**Bijective.** A function is bijective if it is both injective and surjective. This kind of functions are called a *bijection*.<br>
<br>

**Algebraic structure.** operations(e.g., $+,\cdot$), relations(e.g., order), laws(e.g., associativity, commutativity)<br>
**Automorphism.** A map on a set which preserves its algebraic structure. <br>
<br>

**Power.** $(\text{base})^{(\text{exponent})}=(\text{power});$ "$2$ to the power of $6$ is equal to $64.$"<br>
**Root.** $\sqrt[{(\text{index})}]{(\text{radicand})};$ "$\sqrt[6]{64},$ the positive $6$-th root of $64$ is equal to $2$".<br>
<br>

**Composition.** $(f\circ g)(x)$ is read out loud as "$f$ of $g$ at $x,$" or "$f$ of $g$ evaluated at $x.$" <br>
**Derivative.** $[(Df)\circ g](Dg)$ is read out loud as "the derivative of $f$ composed with $g,$ multiplied by the derivative of $g,$" or "the product of the derivative of $f$ composed with $g$ and the derivative of $g.$"<br>
<br>

**Working semantics.** A symbol or expression has working semantics for a subject when the subject can reliably associate it with stable patterns of use, expected inferential moves, typical transformations, and relevant representations, even if the subject is not explicitly thinking about a full interpretation in a model.
