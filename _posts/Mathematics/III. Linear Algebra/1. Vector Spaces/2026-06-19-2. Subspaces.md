**Definition 1.2.1.** A subset $\mathbb W$ of a vector space $\mathbb V$ over a field $\mathbb F$ is called a *subspace* of $\mathbb V$ if $\mathbb W$ is a vector space over $\mathbb F$ with the operations of addition and scalar multiplication defined on $\mathbb V.$<br>
<br>

**Remark 1.2.2.** Let $\mathbb V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb F,$ and $\mathbb W$ a subset of $V.$ Because (VS 1), (VS 2), (VS 5), (VS 6), (VS 7), and (VS 8) hold for all vectors in $\mathbb V,$ these automatically hold for the vectors in $\mathbb W.$ Thus $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V$ if and only if the following four properties are true in $\mathbb W.$ 
1. $\forall u,v\in \mathbb W \ (u+v\in \mathbb W).$
2. $\forall c \in \mathbb F\  \forall u\in \mathbb W \ (cu\in \mathbb W).$
3. $\exists u\in \mathbb W \ [\forall v\in \mathbb W \ (v+u=v)].$ 
4. $\forall u\in \mathbb W \ (-u\in \mathbb W).$

<br>

**Theorem 1.2.3.** Let $\mathbb V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb F,$ and $\mathbb W$ a subset of $V.$ Then $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V$ if and only if the following three properties are true in $\mathbb W.$
1. $\mathit 0\in \mathbb W.$
2. $\forall u,v\in \mathbb W \ (u+v\in \mathbb W).$
3. $\forall c \in \mathbb F\  \forall u\in \mathbb W \ (cu\in \mathbb W).$

*Proof*. Suppose that $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V.$ By Definition 1.2.1, (2) and (3) hold, and there exists $\exists u\in \mathbb W$ such that $\forall v\in \mathbb W \ (v+u=v).$ But in $\mathbb V,$  we have $v+\mathit 0= v,$ and thus, by Theorem 1.1.12, $u=\mathit 0.$ Therefore (1) holds.<br>
&emsp; Conversely, suppose that (1), (2), and (3) hold. Then $(-1)u\in \mathbb W.$ We also have $0u=\mathit 0,$ because $u+0u=1u+0u=(1+0)u=1u=u.$ Thus $u+(-1)u=1u+(-1)u=[1+(-1)]u=0u=\mathit 0.$ Therefore $-u=(-1)u\in \mathbb W.$ From Remark 1.1.2, we can see that $\mathbb W$ is a subspace of $\mathbb V.$ <span class="qed">$\square$</span>
